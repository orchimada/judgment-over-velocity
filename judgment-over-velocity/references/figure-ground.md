# Figure/ground instruments — judging products by the behavior they install

A product's feature list is the *figure* — the thing everyone stares at. The *ground* is the environment the product creates: the workflows it installs, the habits it trains, the dependencies it builds, the things it silently makes easy or hard. Competitors copy figures; grounds are what keep a product a category ahead.

## Instrument 1 — Behavior installed, not features listed

For any product or idea, answer: **after six months of use, what does the user do differently — even when the product is closed?**

- A roadmapping tool's figure is "beautiful timeline views." Its ground might be "PMs now frame everything as outcomes because the tool's template demands a metric per row" — or it might be nothing, in which case the tool is decoration.
- If the behavior installed is "none," the product is replaceable by whatever copies its figure cheaper.
- The installed behavior is the honest unit of differentiation. Lead positioning with it.

Test question for any feature debate: *which candidate installs a behavior, and which merely performs one?*

## Instrument 2 — Abundance manufactures scarcity

Every capability that makes something abundant makes something else scarce. The new scarcity is the next market.

| Made abundant | Made scarce | Who captures it |
|---|---|---|
| Prototypes (AI codegen) | Judgment about what to build | Whoever owns the decision layer |
| Content (generative AI) | Attention, trust, provenance | Whoever verifies/curates |
| Answers (LLMs) | Good questions, problem framing | Whoever frames the problem |
| Features (cheap dev) | Coherence, focus, saying no | Whoever owns strategy discipline |
| Data (instrumentation) | Synthesis, signal-from-noise | Whoever compresses to insight |

When evaluating an idea: name what it makes abundant, then name the scarcity that abundance creates. The durable business is usually anchored to the scarcity, not the abundance. If the idea sells the abundance ("more prototypes, faster!"), flag it — abundances race to zero margin.

## Instrument 3 — Invisible assumptions audit

List every assumption the idea needs to be true. For each, mark:

- **Load-bearing** (idea dies if false) vs **decorative** (idea survives)
- **Stable** (unlikely to change in 24 months) vs **melting** (technology or market trends are actively eroding it)

Classic melting assumptions to check explicitly:
- "This data/skill/output stays expensive" — AI is melting most of these
- "The buyer is the user"
- "Users will keep performing this workflow step manually"
- "The incumbent won't bundle this for free"
- "Integration friction protects us"

Load-bearing + melting = the riskiest assumption. It goes first into Gate 4's disconfirming test.

## Instrument 4 — Seams between domains

Defensible positions usually sit at seams — where two domains meet and neither incumbent owns the problem:

- Between product and finance: connecting roadmap bets to P&L language
- Between research and decision: insight that actually changes a prioritization
- Between AI capability and org design: who does what when agents absorb the junior work
- Between sales promises and product reality: cross-team promise tracking

Check: does the idea sit in a seam (neither side's home turf — good) or in the middle of an incumbent's core (they out-resource you — bad)? Seam positions look small from inside either domain, which is exactly why they're available.

## Instrument 5 — The rear-view mirror check

New capabilities get used first to do the *old* job faster (horseless carriage thinking). Ask: is this idea automating the old workflow, or enabling the behavior the capability newly makes possible?

- Rear-view: "AI writes your PRDs faster" (old artifact, faster)
- Forward: "teams stop writing PRDs because the prototype *is* the spec" (new behavior)

Rear-view ideas can be good businesses but have short half-lives — they die when the workflow they accelerate dies. Flag the half-life explicitly in the verdict.
